# Onboarding Coverage Estimate

This document captures a question about how much of the Sirius source code the
[`onboarding-path.md`](onboarding-path.md) plan actually has you read, and the
answer. Sharing alongside `onboarding-path.md` for review by someone who knows the
codebase well — feedback welcome on whether these estimates and the
spine-plus-deep-dives approach are sound.

---

## Question

> Following this plan, how much actual source code would I end up reading? The
> project is massive, so reading every component would be almost impossible. Am I
> correct in thinking that this plan provides an understanding of the lifetime of a
> query, understanding the source code of some key components, and how they
> interact. Not understanding line by line a massive amount of code. If you were to
> estimate, ignoring the code which is either third-party or autogenerated via make
> and so on, how much of the code would I be reading, either by number of lines or
> percentage of the whole?

---

## Answer

Your framing is exactly correct. The plan is built as a **spine + deep dives**: you
read the full query lifetime end-to-end (the spine), go deep on a handful of
representative components, and *navigate* the rest via docs and the glossary rather
than reading line-by-line. You are explicitly **not** reading the whole engine.

Here are the actual numbers, measured against the tree.

**Baseline:** active source (excluding `src/legacy/` and all third-party submodules)
= **76,506 lines**.

### Tier 1 — Read closely, line by line (the spine + representative operators)

The 12 files the plan names explicitly in Weeks 1–3 sum to **~6,260 lines (~8%)**.
This is the part you'll genuinely understand top to bottom — and it's deliberately
front-loaded with small or shallow files: `sirius_extension.cpp` is large
(~1,700 lines) but mostly registration/config boilerplate that reads quickly, while
the small operators (limit at 129, duckdb_scan at 129) are quick by virtue of size.
The one large *and* algorithmically dense file, hash join (1,224 lines), is deferred
to Week 5. (All counts are raw `wc -l`, including comments and blank lines.)

### Tier 2 — Component deep-dives (Weeks 4–6: read the core, skim the edges)

The directories you dive into total ~24,300 lines, but you won't read them whole.
The big one is the **scan subsystem (~11,700 lines)**, much of which is GPU parquet
decoders the plan tells you to skim, not study. Realistically you read the central
files closely and pass over the rest — call it **~10,000–12,000 lines read closely,
~12,000 skimmed**.

### The estimate

| Engagement level | Lines | % of active source |
|---|---|---|
| **Read closely** (understand the logic) | ~16,000–18,000 | **~21–24%** |
| **+ Skimmed** (eyes over it, get the shape) | ~28,000–30,000 total | **~37–40%** |
| **Never touched** | ~46,000 | ~60% |

So: **you'll closely read roughly a fifth to a quarter of the active codebase
(~16–18k lines), and meaningfully expose yourself to ~40%.** The remaining ~60% —
`src/io`, `src/telemetry`, `src/transparent`, `src/util`, `src/data`,
`src/scan_manager`, the bulk of `src/cuda/`, etc. — you'll only open when a specific
task drags you there.

A few honest caveats on those numbers:

- **They're a planned floor, not a ceiling.** Real debugging always pulls you into
  files the plan didn't list. Expect the "closely read" figure to drift up by a few
  thousand lines just from chasing things in practice — that's healthy, not plan
  failure.
- **"Closely read" ≠ "mastered."** You'll truly *own* maybe the ~6k spine plus
  whatever component your eventual contributions live in — call it **~10–15%
  mastered**. The rest is "I've read this and could find my way back," which is the
  right level for most of an engine this size.
- **Headers inflate the line count.** A good chunk of those 76.5k lines are `.hpp`
  declarations (29k across the whole tree); reading a header is much faster than
  reading logic, so your *time* per line is uneven — the spine and the
  concurrency/memory `.cpp` files are where the hours actually go.

The key insight: in a 76k-line engine, **comprehension of how the pieces interact
matters far more than coverage.** Reading 100% would be both impossible on this
timeline and unnecessary — a contributor who deeply understands the query lifecycle
and one or two subsystems is productive; one who skimmed every file but can't trace
a query is not.
